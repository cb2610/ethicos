Philosophy · The ideas behind the work

Practical wisdom for an age of artificial intelligence.

Ethicos AI is grounded in a simple claim: better AI requires better judgement. Not faster models. Not broader adoption. Not longer policy documents. The work of responsible AI is the work of practical wisdom, applied to systems that increasingly act in the world.

The serious question is not whether AI can be made to follow rules. It is what happens to organisations, decisions, and people when AI begins to act in the world — and how we make those actions ones we can stand behind.

This page sets out the ideas that shape how Ethicos approaches AI strategy, ethics, and governance. The work draws on published research in moral reasoning, decision-making, interpretability, and AI safety. It is written for non-specialists.

§ 01 / The interpretation problem
Rules · goals · principles

Rules do not interpret themselves.

Every rule, goal, or principle has to be applied to a case that was not in the rulebook. This is true of laws, contracts, ethical codes, internal policies, and the instructions we give to AI systems. The hard work is rarely in the writing. It is in the moment a rule meets the world and someone has to decide what it means here, in this particular situation, for these particular people.

The same is true of the goals we give to AI. A system can satisfy a goal in a way that is technically correct and substantively wrong: optimising the letter of an instruction while breaking the value it was meant to protect. Anyone who has watched a model learn to game a metric, or a policy be applied to a case nothing like the one it was written for, has met this in person.

"AI governance cannot rely on instructions alone. The hard question is how rules, goals, and values are interpreted when systems meet the world."

The interpretation problem · Badea & Artus, SGAI 2022

This is the interpretation problem. It is not a flaw to be patched out of AI. It is a property of any system that takes rules and applies them. What changes between a thoughtful human reader, a junior employee, and a deep neural network is not whether interpretation happens. It is who can interpret well, how their interpretations can be checked, and what we do when those interpretations diverge.

In practice

What this changes

Responsible AI is not just about writing better rules. It is about designing systems, organisations, and review processes that can interpret, challenge, and correct how rules and values are applied.

Commercial translation

An internal AI policy that cannot be applied to a real edge case is not a policy. Ethicos helps organisations build review processes around the moments of interpretation, not just the principles they are supposed to express.

§ 02 / The law of interpretative exposure
Risk · context · causal reach

The more an AI system can affect the world, the more it matters how it is governed.

Not every AI system carries the same risk. A chatbot drafting internal text, a clinical decision-support tool, a credit model, and an autonomous control system stand in different relationships to the world. Each interprets its inputs in its own way, and each delivers its interpretation back with a different amount of causal force.

A useful way to put this: risk scales with interpretative exposure. The more causally connected a system is to the world, the larger the consequence of any misinterpretation or value failure. This proposition has been articulated in published work as the Artus – Badea Law, sometimes called the Law of Interpretative Exposure. It does not say AI is uniquely dangerous. It says that AI, like every other rule-applying system, carries risk in proportion to how far its interpretations travel.

"The more an AI system can affect the world, the more carefully its assumptions, values, and decision pathways need to be governed."

The Artus – Badea Law · Law of Interpretative Exposure

For institutions, this means governance should not be uniform. A general AI-use policy is not enough for a clinical product, and a clinical-grade review is overkill for a knowledge-base summariser. The work is to match the depth of oversight to the depth of exposure.

In practice

Right-sized governance

Ethicos engagements typically start by mapping a portfolio of AI systems against their interpretative exposure: causal reach, sensitivity of context, reversibility of action. Governance and review then scale to that map, not to a single corporate template.

Where this lands

A board AI policy that scales review to risk is taken more seriously than one that treats every system the same. The exposure lens is also a useful framing for regulators and funders.

§ 03 / Values as vehicles of meaning
Value-based AI · explicit moral reasoning

Values are not decoration. They are the bridge between formal rules and human meaning.

A response to the interpretation problem is to be explicit about the values a system is meant to serve, not only the rules it is meant to follow. Rules describe what to do. Values describe why those rules exist, and which way to lean when the rules run out or pull against each other.

This is the position behind value-based AI: that intelligent systems should make their moral reasoning explicit rather than have it folded silently into weights, parameters, or default behaviours. Making values explicit does not solve the interpretation problem. It changes who is doing the interpreting, and exposes that work to review.

"Value-based AI asks a harder question: not only what did the system decide, but what did that decision mean for the humans affected by it?"

Organisations should not only ask whether an AI system has followed a policy. They should ask what values that system is in fact serving, how those values are represented, what trade-offs are being made on their behalf, and whether humans can inspect and contest the result.

In practice

Beyond rule-following

Audits and ethics reviews can stop at: did the system follow the policy? Better ones ask: which values did its decisions serve, and which did they trade away?

Commercial translation

Many AI products optimise for metrics that quietly stand in for values their teams have not made explicit. Ethicos helps surface what the metric is actually serving and whether the organisation can stand behind it.

§ 04 / Operational values
From principle to commitment

A value becomes useful when it can be connected to people, actions, and consequences.

"Fairness," "safety," and "transparency" are real values, but they are not yet useful claims. They become useful when an organisation can answer four questions about them: fair to whom, against what, observable how, and contestable by whom. Until those questions are answered, the principle is decoration.

The published research has a more formal name for this idea: transactional or anthropo-relational values – values that can be tied to human effects, relationships, and measurable change. In client work we use the plainer label: operational values. The phrase keeps the idea close to the work it is meant to do.

"Ethicos treats values as operational commitments: claims about what should matter, to whom, and in which decisions."

An operational value carries five things with it: a constituency (who it is for), a target (what it changes), an indicator (how it can be seen), a review point (who checks it), and a remedy (what happens when it fails). A principle that lacks any of these is a slogan with good intentions.

In practice

The five-part test

For every value an AI system is meant to express, Ethicos engagements look for: constituency, target, indicator, review point, remedy. The gaps usually map cleanly to the work that still needs doing.

Where this lands

Operational values give a board, a funder, or a regulator something they can actually examine. They make a responsible-AI claim auditable without making it bureaucratic.

§ 05 / The Three Rs
A lens for AI & institutional decisions

Before asking whether a decision was right, ask what was noticed, how it was represented, and how it was reasoned through.

Most analyses of AI decisions begin too late. They start with the output and ask whether it was correct. A more useful lens, drawn from work on meta-decision-making, asks three questions earlier in the chain: what did the system consider relevant, how did it represent that information, and how did it reason from those representations to a conclusion?

R
Relevance

What matters in this situation?

What did the system attend to, and what did it ignore? Many AI failures are failures of relevance long before they are failures of reasoning.

R
Representation

How is what matters represented?

A datum is never neutral. A diagnosis as a label, a feature, a probability, or a sentence is four different things to an AI system.

R
Reasoning

How are representations used to reach a decision?

Two systems with the same inputs can reach opposite conclusions because they reason differently. Interpretability lives here.

The Three Rs are not a methodology. They are a place to look. Most serious problems in AI decision-making sit in one of these three spots, and most useful interventions land there too.

In practice

A design and audit lens

The Three Rs work as a structuring device for product reviews, audits, and governance discussions. They help teams articulate what they are actually delegating to a model, and where the room for disagreement lies.

Where this lands

Used early, they reframe the AI conversation away from "is the model good?" toward "what is the model being asked to do, with what inputs, and reasoning how?" Questions every stakeholder can hold an opinion on.

§ 06 / From values to decisions
Explicit moral reasoning · MARS

Values matter only when they change decisions.

A value that does not survive contact with a hard case is, for practical purposes, not a value the organisation holds. The same is true of the values an AI system is meant to embody. They need to be visible in the decisions the system makes, not only in the documents that describe it.

MARS – the Multi-valued Action Reasoning System – is a research framework for AI systems that deliberate over moral rules explicitly rather than absorbing them implicitly into model behaviour. It is not the answer to every AI ethics question. It is one demonstration that values, actions, impacts, and moral paradigms can be modelled as first-class objects an AI system reasons over, and a human can inspect.

"Values matter only when they change decisions. Ethicos helps organisations move from stated principles to decision structures that can be used, tested, and improved."

After Badea · Multi-valued Action Reasoning System, SGAI 2022

For clients, the lesson from MARS is not "adopt a logic-based AI". It is that explicit moral reasoning is possible, and that organisations should expect the AI systems they deploy in high-stakes contexts to be able to show their reasoning, not just their answer.

In practice

Explicit, inspectable, contestable

Engagements that draw on MARS-style thinking typically focus on three properties: making the system's value commitments explicit, making its reasoning inspectable, and giving humans a way to contest the result.

Where this lands

Particularly useful for clinical, regulatory, and safety-critical contexts, where "the model said so" is not a defensible answer.

§ 07 / Practical wisdom in institutions
Phronēsis · the work itself

Practical wisdom is the condition under which most AI decisions are actually made.

The Greek word for this is phronēsis: practical wisdom, the ability to act well when principles, evidence, incentives, institutions, and human consequences are tangled together. It is the form of judgement Aristotle thought most relevant to ethics – not the deduction of right action from a first principle, but the disciplined exercise of judgement in conditions that are rarely clean.

That description fits almost every serious AI decision an organisation will make in the next decade. Should we deploy this model? What is the threshold for human review? Who owns the failure modes? When does an improvement to accuracy justify a reduction in interpretability? These are not problems that yield to a checklist. They are problems that yield to careful reasoning, applied with judgement, by people who understand both the system and the institution it lives in.

"Responsible AI is not solved by slogans, checklists, or abstract principles alone. It asks for clear reasoning, institutional judgement, and design choices that hold up under pressure."

That is what Ethicos AI exists to support. Not the production of more principles. The application of practical wisdom — phronēsis — to the AI decisions that organisations are actually making, with the technical, philosophical, and institutional depth those decisions deserve.

In practice

The work itself

Ethicos engagements are designed to leave clients better at this kind of judgement: clearer about what is at stake, more articulate about their values, more disciplined about how decisions get made and reviewed.

Where this lands

The best engagements end with a team that no longer needs an outside ethicist for every decision — they have built the practice in-house. That is the right success condition.
